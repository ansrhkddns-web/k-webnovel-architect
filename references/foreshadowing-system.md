# Foreshadowing System

Use this file to design and track long-term setups, mysteries, reversals, and payoffs.

## Types Of Foreshadowing

- `mystery`: An unanswered question the reader wants solved.
- `power`: A hidden rule, skill ceiling, condition, or cost.
- `identity`: Secret birth, past life, hidden role, erased memory, disguised figure.
- `relationship`: A bond, betrayal, contract, affection, debt, or promise that changes meaning.
- `world`: A false history, hidden faction, broken law of nature, sealed disaster.
- `threat`: An enemy or disaster hinted before it appears directly.
- `theme`: Repeated motif that later becomes a choice or reversal.

## Foreshadowing Ledger

Track each item with:

```markdown
| ID | Type | Planted | Surface Meaning | Hidden Truth | Reminder Cadence | Payoff Target | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| F-001 | mystery | Ep. 1 |  |  | every 5-10 eps | Ep. 20-25 | open |
```

Status values:
- `planned`: Intended but not planted.
- `open`: Planted and still unresolved.
- `complicated`: Partially answered but made deeper.
- `paid`: Paid off.
- `dropped`: Remove or repurpose before it feels like a broken promise.

## Payoff Ranges

Use different ranges for different promises:

- `Immediate`: 1-3 episodes. Useful for action tactics, small lies, minor threats.
- `Short arc`: 4-10 episodes. Useful for first enemy, first skill condition, first relationship shift.
- `Major arc`: 10-25 episodes. Useful for faction secrets, revenge target, identity hints.
- `Season scale`: 25-50 episodes. Useful for world truth, main antagonist layer, protagonist origin.
- `Series scale`: 50+ episodes. Use sparingly for central myth or final enemy.

## Fairness Rules

- Plant before payoff.
- Make the clue visible enough that readers can remember it when reminded.
- Let the surface meaning make sense before the hidden truth is revealed.
- Pay off some promises early so readers trust long-term promises.
- Avoid stacking too many unresolved mysteries without emotional or material rewards.

## Layered Reveal Pattern

Use this for long mysteries:

1. `Odd detail`: Something feels slightly off.
2. `Practical use`: The odd detail helps or hurts in a scene.
3. `False answer`: A plausible explanation appears.
4. `Contradiction`: The false answer fails.
5. `Partial truth`: Reader gets a real answer but not the whole truth.
6. `Cost`: The truth changes the protagonist's choices.
7. `Full payoff`: The original clue is reinterpreted.

## Common Problems

### Mystery Without Reward

Problem: The story only withholds answers.

Fix:
- Add small tactical answers while preserving the large mystery.
- Let clues produce immediate gains, danger, or relationship change.

### Payoff Too Late

Problem: Readers forget or stop caring.

Fix:
- Add reminders.
- Move a partial reveal earlier.
- Tie the mystery to the protagonist's current goal.

### Twist Without Setup

Problem: The reveal surprises but does not feel earned.

Fix:
- Add at least two earlier clues with innocent surface meanings.
- Make a character's earlier behavior reinterpret cleanly.

### Too Many Secrets

Problem: The plan becomes foggy.

Fix:
- Keep one central mystery and two active secondary mysteries per arc.
- Pay off or retire old questions before opening new large ones.
